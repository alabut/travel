The Anti-Tourist's Guide

To

Buenos Aires

Hi, I'm Kate Click! I lived in Argentina for a year and these are my notes from my time there. Many say exploration is part of our destiny, but it’s actually our duty to future generations and their quest to ensure survival.

Neighborhoods

Plaza Armenia is a particularly pleasant place to be on weekends, as it has all the charm of local drum teams and merchants without the noise of tourists and trinket peddlers. Grab a bottle of wine, lay in the grass, and watch the world go by.

Palermo - The biggest neighborhood in the city, Palermo is the home base for local yuppies and foreign expats. Worth a visit for its solid shopping and restaurants, Palermo also has its share of tourist traps (cough…Plaza Serrano…cough). Stay on the edges of the neighborhood in areas like Palermo Soho, Palermo Hollywood, or Palermo Chico to get a more local sense of the place.

Villa Crespo - lies south of Cordoba opposite Palermo, and is its larger neighbor's more mellow counterpart. Locals head here for its outlet shops and quaint cafes without the touristy prices and noise of more mainstream areas.

Almagro - Amalgro is a true locals neighborhood and a fairly accurate representation of everyday life in middle class BA. It's also home to Bomba del Tiempo, an improv drum show that takes place every Monday night and draws hoards of travelers and young locals, alike.

Recoleta: Home to the famed cemetario of the same name (see below), Recoleta is home to monied (likely older) tourists, old-money aristocrats, and international embassies. See it for a glimpse at what BA was in its 'Paris of the South' golden years, but don't stay for the overhyped and overpriced shops. You'll get better bargains and a more authentic sense of the city elsewhere.

Restaurants

Don Julio: Locals skip the touristy La Cabrera and go here--better steak and atmosphere

Crizia: delicious food and upscale w/o being pretentious

Olsen: Scandinavian food; Great, but especially for Sunday brunch

Tegui: Swank spot behind a graffiti facade; sister restaurant to Olsen

Sarkis: No frills, killer Armenian.

Dadá: Great bar/bistro hole in the wall with lots of character (in Microcentro)

Name of the place - gravida at eget metus. Duis mollis, est non commodo.

Bars

Frank's: speakeasy style bar with the best cocktails in BA; getting in is a bit kitschy (password required as revealed by clues on their Facebook and you enter through a phone booth), which sounds ridiculous but it's worth it

Congo: nice spot with great outdoor space, especially in summer

Doppelganger: San Telmo spot with good martinis

Post St. Bar: Dingy expat dive, but worth stopping by for a Quilmes just to see a collage of graffiti from local street artists

Things To Do

Feria de San Telmo: Sunday antiques market; can be touristy/crowded, but a must see

Anuva Wine Tasting

Plaza Armenia: I lived on this plaza and there was always something going on Saturdays/Sundays. If the weather is nice, grab a bottle of wine, hang out on the grass, and watch the world go by.

Cemetario de la Recoleta

La Catedral: 'underground' milonga in Amalgro, pay a small cover at the door and go for tango lessons or late after dinner (a la 1am) to see a show (If I could go back to one place, it would be here)

La Viruta: authentic, local milonga; great for classes or just watching locals

Palacio Barolo: the whole building is an allegory of Dante's Inferno; interesting tours (though offered at tricky times) and great shots at the top of the lighthouse if you're a shutterbug

Teatro Colon: iconic; looks like there's a Tchaikovsky ballet playing now

Graffitimundo: BA is covered in street art and this tour goes to some notable displays and gives background on the artists

Things to Note:

  • Be aware of surroundings when walking home late; there were a few times I separated from a group of loud, English-speaking (shouting) expat friends and was followed home.
  • All cabs will be black and yellow, but some will have official company signage and others will be unmarked. When possible, hail only taxis with official Radio Taxi (or any other official taxi company) lights/signage. You won't be mugged in an unmarked cab, but if they might take you around the block an extra time.
  • If you go to dinner before 10pm, you and any other Americans will be the only ones in the restaurant. Not that there's anything wrong with that.
  • Watch out for fake bills from cab drivers or vendors.
